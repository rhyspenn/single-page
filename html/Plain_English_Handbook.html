What is Plain English?

Plain English does not mean deleting complex information. It ensures the orderly and clear presentation of complex information so investors have the best possible chance of understanding it. [cite: 68, 70]

Writing in Plain English

Focus on clear, concise language to avoid common problems like long sentences, passive voice, weak verbs, jargon, and unnecessary details. [cite: 192]

Active Voice

Strong Verbs

Use active voice with strong verbs for clarity and conciseness. The subject performs the action (e.g., "The investor buys the stock"). [cite: 207, 208, 217] Avoid passive voice ("The stock is bought") unless necessary. [cite: 218, 242] Find hidden verbs in nouns (nominalizations). [cite: 243, 244]

Use Pronouns

You & We

Use personal pronouns ("you," "we") to speak directly to readers, clarify roles, avoid abstractions, shorten sentences, and bypass gender issues. [cite: 251, 252, 253, 254, 255]

Be Concrete

Avoid Abstractions

Replace abstract financial terms with concrete ones or use examples/scenarios to help readers visualize concepts. [cite: 261, 264, 267, 274] Move from general to specific (Asset -> Stock -> IBM Stock). [cite: 278]

Cut Clutter

Concise Language

Eliminate words that can be replaced with fewer words meaning the same thing (e.g., "in order to" -> "to"). [cite: 284, 285] Avoid "shotgunning" lists of synonyms; pick the best word. [cite: 285, 286]

Write Positively

Avoid Negatives

Positive sentences are shorter and clearer (e.g., "Only the beneficiary may receive..." vs. "Persons other than... may not receive..."). [cite: 298, 299] Replace negative phrases with single positive words (e.g., "not certain" -> "uncertain"). [cite: 301]

Short Sentences

Break it Down

Avoid very long, complex sentences packed with information, jargon, and legalese. Break them down for better reader comprehension. [cite: 303, 304, 305] Provide context before details. [cite: 309]

Drop Jargon

Use Common Words

Ruthlessly eliminate legal and financial jargon. Use short, common words. If a technical term is necessary, explain it on first use. [cite: 331, 332, 333] Avoid creating new acronyms. [cite: 336]

Simple Words

Choose Simpler Synonyms

When a shorter, simpler synonym exists, use it (e.g., "end" not "terminate", "use" not "utilize"). [cite: 351, 352, 353]

Natural Order

Subject-Verb-Object

Keep the subject, verb, and object close together, following the natural SVO word order. Avoid interrupting with long modifiers. [cite: 356, 359, 360]

Use If-Then

Conditionals

Structure conditional statements clearly using "if-then." Handle multiple conditions logically, possibly using tabulation or tables. [cite: 367, 369, 370, 371, 372, 373, 374]

Parallelism

Consistent Structure

Ensure items in a list or series use parallel grammatical form (e.g., all nouns or all verbs) for clarity and rhythm. [cite: 376, 377]

Avoid "Respectively"

Clarity Over Brevity

This word forces readers to backtrack and match items, increasing effort. Rewrite sentences to state relationships directly. [cite: 389, 392, 393]

Designing the Document

Good design makes information easier to read and understand. Focus on hierarchy, typography, layout, graphics, and color to serve the goal of clear communication. [cite: 397, 398, 402]

Hierarchy

Levels of Info

Use distinct styles (typeface, size, weight) for headings and text to show information structure (e.g., Title -> Section -> Subsection -> Text). Limit levels (max 6 recommended). [cite: 413, 414, 415]

Typography

Typefaces & Size

Choose readable typefaces. Serif fonts (like Times, Garamond) are generally better for body text; sans-serif (like Helvetica, Arial) for headings. [cite: 419, 420, 422, 431] Use appropriate point sizes (10-12pt common for text). [cite: 455] Use bold/italics sparingly for emphasis; avoid all caps. [cite: 459, 461, 464] Limit to 2 typefaces per document. [cite: 448]

Spacing & Alignment

Use Wisely
